Hacking Devices and Software

This semester, I am taking CIT425/L alongside the rest of my courses. CIT425 is a Information and Systems Security class taught by Dr. Kaplan. In the class we explore a broad selection of systems security issues, concepts, and policies with topics within the principles of information systems security that include cryptography, identifications and authentications, access control models and mechanisms, multilevel database security, Internet security, risk analysis, and social issues that include the role privacy plays in our everyday lives in a society that has become heavily reliant on technology. In this week's blog, I will discuss the importance of taking preventative measures.

The Awaited Release

In last week's blogpost, I discussed the release of the Apple's new iPhone 12 lineup and coincidentally, my group and I received the topic of "Hacking a Smartphone" in our CIT425 Information and Systems Security class. The presentation went over how far the technology industry has come with smartphone innovations since the creation of the first telephone in 1876. Today, in the 21st century, more than 3.5 billion individuals have their own mini (but definitely powerful) computer right at the convenience of their pockets.

With the amount of smartphones out in the world right now, we have become as efficient as ever! With instantaneous directions at the touch of a button, easy access to multiple methods of payment, and limitless storage of private data, the public is able to streamline their day to day activities. However, with these benefits, must come great caution. Due to the nature of today's technology, almost anything can be accessed with malicious intent.

All devices connected to the internet can be hacked into. And on another level, anything on the internet (ie. social media, bank, cloud accounts, etc.) can also be penetrated. Therefore, it is with utmost importance that preventitive measures are taken to ensure the safety and resillience of devices and accounts.
